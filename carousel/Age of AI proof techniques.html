Bring back oral and written exams

Take back home type of grading is vulnerable to AI exploits, so what better way to test competency than to see them perform in real life? Students get prepared for the exam and make sure they are well-learned. Then form their knowledge creatively to get tested fairly. It's also worth noting that producing such outputs makes learning more memorable.

Focus on analytical skills too

Instead of grading the brick of text, think outside the box and grade its relation to other sources. For example, have them attach all the copies of academic sources they read and cited from, and assess how they used the writings and references. This allows learners to actually engage with the assignment itself in a fair way, while also improving their ability to use references.

Incorporating in-class discussion to the evaluation process itself

AI does not have a knowledge of what happened in the classroom, but the learner does. Ask learners questions like:

  • What was the most surprising thing you learned in today's class? Why?
  • How did today's content connect to a prior lecture or discussion?
  • Imagine you're explaining today's topic to a friend who missed class. What 3 key points would you mention, and why?
  • Was there any moment in today's session where you felt confused or intrigued? Describe it.

As a bonus, these self-reflection type of questions, instead of rigid, uncreative ones helps learners to internalize the knowledge. You can use them as homeworks, essays or during the closing discussion of a lecture.

Ask for the research process itself

AI, notorious for its occasional 'hallucinations', can't show the steps used to do research. When students are working on such assignment, they will not only produce a writing but also be conscious of the way they research. To achieve this you can simply add similar questions to the instructions section:

  • Which keywords did you use during your research, and why did you choose them?
  • How did you evaluate whether a source was trustworthy or not? Give an example.
  • Was there a source you ended up discarding? Why?
  • What was the hardest part of the research process for you personally? Why?
